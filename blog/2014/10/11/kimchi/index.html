Mak Kimchi

There are two things I’ve learned about Kimchi, or pickles in general: you can make them with any vegetable, and you can eat them on anything.

My favorite things to eat kimchi with? Toasted sour dough bread with butter, feta cheese, and a mountain of kimchi. Or even better: mozzarella sticks.

Adapted from Modernist Cuisine to be a bit simpler. The original recipe used the more traditional approach of creating a pepper paste and spreading it in between each leaf of a napa cabbage. This is a bit difficult to do with a dense savoy. I also, its convenient to chop up the ingredients before fermentation so you don’t need cover your cutting board with all lacto-goodness before serving.

Myhrvold and friends also propose a 24 week fermentation. Half a year is a bit too long of a turn over for me. Feel free to eat this right after you made it. It’ll be light much lighter and taste more like a salad than a pickle. Depending on how hot your house is, it’ll be sour in about three days to a week. Put it in the fridge to slow down fermentation. However, there are some great dishes to be made from heavily fermented and sour kimchi (kimchi jjigae (kimchi stew), kimchi bokkeumbap (kimchi fried rice)).

Fermentation can be done in a facny earthern crock with a weight to hold the vegetables down, or simply a plastic tub. The salty solution that the kimchi lives in for the first few days prevents other not so friendly bacteria from colonizing before the lacto-bacteria can colonize. As a result, if after the first few days the kimchi is not covered with liquid, try pressing it down to get out some of the air. If you still cannot get it covered, add a bit of a one percent brine by weight until it is covered.

Finally, if your container is very well sealed, be sure to check on your creation every day or so: it will be releasing gas and, as we all know from experience, pent up gas can lead to explosions.

Recipe

The procedure is simple: first brine the vegetables so they release some of their water, take them out of the brine, coat them in a fishy pepper paste, and wait.

Use whatever vegetables you’d like and see if it works. Cabbage is classic.

Brine

  • 2% brine solution by weight

That is, for every 1 kg of water, add 20 g of salt. You’ll need enough to cover your vegetables

Pepper Paste

All ingredients are by weight relative to the amount of vegetables. For example, if you have 1200 g of cabbage, add 144 g (0.14 * 1200 = 144) garlic.

  • 12% garlic, finely minced
  • 8% fish sauce
  • 7.5% light soy sauce
  • 6% Korean chilli powder (gochugaru
  • 4.5% scallions, minced
  • 4% ginger, finely minced
  • 4% carrot, julienne
  • 1% dried salted shrimp (optional)

Procedure

  1. Chop up vegetables into whatever sized pieces you would like to server (for example, bite sized).
  2. Place vegetables in a large bowl (or multiple) and cover liberally with brine
  3. Allow the vegetables to soak for an hour or so. If you remember, come back and give ‘em a bit of a stir
  4. Drain the brine, return vegetables to large bowl
  5. Combine vegetables with paste (use your hands, watch out for clumps)
  6. Transfer to your pickling container

Taste some right afterwards. It will be light, slightly fishy, and fairly sweet. Observe it as it ferments and notice the increase in the mild lactic acid and the softening of the vegetables.
